# Adaptation — Internal Teams (Community Pods, AI Champions, Learning Cohorts)

For running this curriculum inside your own organization — community-of-practice pods, AI Champions programs, departmental learning cohorts.

---

## What's different about internal cohorts

- **Audience is consistent.** You'll see the same people across multiple sessions. Build for arc, not for one-shot.
- **Scenarios are easy to source.** Real internal workflows are everywhere. Use them.
- **Peer pressure works.** Cohort dynamics — visible progress, peer demos, shared backlog — drive adoption harder than facilitator energy.
- **Time is scarce but recurring.** Don't fight for a 4-hour event. Win 60 minutes every two weeks for 6 months.

---

## The community pod format (recommended)

A 6-month commitment with a small cohort (8–15 people) and a recurring 60-minute biweekly session.

| Cadence | Session |
|---|---|
| **Week 1** | Track 0 L100 — Responsible AI |
| **Week 3** | Track 1 L100 — Copilot CLI |
| **Week 5** | Track 3 L100 — Prompt Engineering |
| **Week 7** | Track 2 L100 — Building Agents |
| **Week 9** | Track 5 L100 — Memory & Tokens |
| **Week 11** | Track 6 Topic 2 L100 — Workflow Design |
| **Week 13–24** | Cohort picks L200/L300 deep-dives based on what they actually need |

**Why this works:** the off-weeks (between sessions) are where adoption happens. Each session ends with a homework: *"try this in your real work this week, demo it next session."* Peer accountability does the rest.

## The AI Champions variant

For a Champions program (peer mentors who get a small time allocation to support adoption):

- Champions go through the **full curriculum L100 + L200** as a cohort over ~3 months
- Champions then **run L100 sessions for their own teams** as part of the program
- Monthly champion-only debriefs: what's working, what's not, what's missing

The curriculum becomes the **shared vocabulary** of the Champions program. New champions onboard via the same path. The program scales without re-inventing materials each time.

## Sourcing internal scenarios

The single biggest advantage of internal cohorts is access to real workflows. Don't waste it.

**Three reliable scenario sources:**

1. **Cohort intake survey.** Ask every cohort member to describe one workflow they spend more than 2 hours/week on. Use the most common one as the recurring scenario across sessions.
2. **Their managers.** Ask managers what they wish their team did more efficiently. Often surfaces workflows the cohort members themselves overlooked.
3. **Recent incidents or escalations.** Real failures are great teaching material. With permission and anonymization, work through "what would AI have changed here?" in the relevant session.

See [scenarios/README.md](../scenarios/README.md) for guidance on writing reusable scenarios.

## Cohort metrics that matter

Don't measure attendance. Measure:

| Metric | How to capture |
|---|---|
| **Pre-work completion rate** | Microsoft Learn completion API or honor-system survey |
| **In-session activation** | "Did you try the technique live in this session?" 1-question poll |
| **30-day usage** | Light-touch follow-up: "Did you use this in real work this week?" |
| **90-day workflow change** | Specific: "Has any recurring workflow in your role changed because of this curriculum?" |
| **Cohort-level demos** | Number of cohort members who demoed their own work to peers |

The last one — **peer demos** — is the leading indicator of program health. A cohort where members are demoing to each other unprompted is succeeding. A cohort where the facilitator demos every time is not.

---

## What success looks like for internal cohorts

- **30-day:** 70%+ of the cohort tried at least one technique in real work
- **90-day:** at least 3 cohort members have made a measurable workflow change they can articulate
- **End of program:** the cohort has produced 3+ scenarios you can publish to the [scenarios/](../scenarios/) library
- **Long-term:** alumni of the cohort become facilitators of the next cohort. That's the loop.

---

## Common pitfalls

1. **Recruiting the cohort too broadly.** A cohort of 50 is a webinar. A cohort of 8–15 is a learning community. Pick the smaller number.
2. **Letting attendance lapse without consequence.** If cohort members can skip 3 of 6 sessions, they will. Set an expectation up front: "Attend 5 of 6, do the homework, demo at least once. Otherwise let someone else have the seat."
3. **No alumni path.** Cohorts that end without an "ok, what now?" lose 80% of their long-tail value. Have a Champions program, advanced cohort, or contribution path ready to offer to high-engagement alumni.

## Light-touch variant (60 minutes total)

Don't have 6 months? Run a single **Track 0 + Track 1 L100 combo** as a one-off lunch-and-learn. Manage expectations: this is exposure, not enablement. Offer the full cohort program as the next step for anyone who wants more.

This is also the right format for a "do we want to do the full cohort?" pitch session to leadership.
