# Adaptation — Partner Enablement (Train-the-Trainer)

For partner trainers, partner program managers, and field SEs running enablement sessions where the goal is *the partner can run this themselves afterward*.

---

## What's different about partner enablement

- **The audience is a future facilitator,** not an end learner. They need to understand *why* each step exists, not just what to do.
- **You're optimizing for transfer, not for delivery.** A partner who runs the curriculum 10 times for their customers is worth far more than a single great workshop.
- **Materials matter more than charisma.** Partners don't have you with them when they run the session. They have your runsheet and slides.

---

## The 4-hour partner enablement format

A half-day session that produces a partner who can run **Track 0 L100 + Track 1 L100** for their own customers.

| Hour | Activity |
|---|---|
| **Hour 1** | Run Track 0 L100 *as a participant*. Partner experiences the session as a learner first. |
| **Hour 2** | Walk through the **facilitator notes** for Track 0 L100. Why each step exists. Where partners commonly stumble. Q&A. |
| **Hour 3** | Run Track 1 L100 *as a participant*. Same pattern. |
| **Hour 4** | Walk through the Track 1 L100 facilitator notes. Hand off the materials. Schedule follow-up. |

**Why the as-a-participant + as-a-facilitator split:** partners who only see the facilitator view never internalize the learner experience. Partners who only see the learner view can't run the session well. You need both.

## Building a partner trainer cohort

Don't enable one partner trainer in isolation. The model that works:

1. **Cohort of 5–10 partner trainers** from different partners (or different geos within a large partner). Run the 4-hour enablement once.
2. **30-day check-in** — each trainer reports on their first run. The group debriefs. This is where the curriculum gets real-world feedback.
3. **90-day check-in** — measurable outcomes. Which trainers ran how many sessions? What worked? What failed?
4. **Annual refresh** — Microsoft Learn modules drift. The curriculum drifts. Re-enable the cohort once a year.

The cohort model creates peer accountability and a shared improvement backlog. Solo enablement rarely produces sustained delivery.

## What partners need that direct customers don't

- **Pricing/packaging context** for the Microsoft tools referenced (Copilot, Copilot Studio, Azure AI Foundry). Partners get asked this in every customer session. Brief them.
- **Common customer objections and responses.** Partners who only have product training stumble on "but doesn't this leak our data?" — Track 0 answers this, but only if the partner can articulate it confidently.
- **A path to escalate.** When a customer asks a question the partner can't answer, who do they call? Make this explicit.

---

## What success looks like for partner enablement

- **30-day:** each enabled partner has run at least one customer session
- **90-day:** each enabled partner has run 3+ customer sessions, with 30-day adoption follow-ups for each
- **Long-term:** partners contribute their own scenarios and adaptations back to this repo. That's the loop that makes this curriculum durable.

---

## Common pitfalls

1. **Skipping the as-a-participant hour.** "They've used Copilot, they don't need the L100 experience." Wrong — they need the *facilitator* experience of L100, which only comes from re-experiencing it intentionally.
2. **No follow-up cadence.** Partner enablement without 30/90-day check-ins is a one-shot training. Partners forget what they don't practice.
3. **Letting partners default to Microsoft sales decks.** This curriculum is *learning content*, not pitch content. If partners blend the two, you'll get vendor-pitch sessions and the customers will disengage.

---

## Co-delivery option

For high-priority partners, consider **co-delivering the first customer session together.** The partner facilitates the front of the room; you backstop hard questions. After one co-delivered session, the partner runs solo with a 24-hour debrief. This is the highest-investment, highest-yield enablement path.
